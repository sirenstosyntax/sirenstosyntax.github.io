## From Emergency Response to AI Engineering

Over 20+ years in the fire service, I've progressed from firefighter (truck and engine companies) to hazardous materials technician (15+ years), training division instructor (technical rescue and hazmat response), and now fire captain. I've spent my career responding to emergencies, leading teams under pressure, and making split-second decisions that protect lives and property. It's been an incredible journey serving my community, but as my fire service career winds down, I'm preparing to embark on a new challenge that excites me just as much: artificial intelligence engineering.

## Why AI?

I've become increasingly fascinated by AI - both its tremendous promise and the critical challenges it poses. The questions around AI safety, alignment, and responsible development remind me of the safety protocols and risk management that are fundamental to firefighting. Just as we plan for worst-case scenarios in emergency services, I believe we need the same rigor and foresight in developing AI systems.

## Why the Parallels Matter

The overlap between emergency services and AI development runs deeper than it might first appear:

**Understanding the actual problems** — I've experienced firsthand the pain points that plague emergency response: training systems that don't reflect real-world complexity, operational workflows that create friction under pressure, and documentation requirements that pull focus from critical tasks. This isn't theoretical knowledge from user interviews—it's two decades of living with these challenges daily. I know where the friction points are because I've felt them at 3 AM on a hazmat scene.

**Risk mitigation mindset** — Fifteen years as a hazmat technician taught me to think in failure modes, cascading effects, and layers of protection. In AI safety, these same principles apply: anticipating edge cases, planning for unintended consequences, and building robust systems that fail gracefully.

**Teaching complex concepts** — As a training division instructor for technical rescue and hazmat response, I've learned to break down intricate procedures into understandable frameworks. This skill translates directly to explaining model decisions, documenting systems, and bridging the gap between technical teams and stakeholders.

**Decision-making under uncertainty** — Emergency scenes rarely offer complete information. You assess what you know, identify what matters most, and adapt as situations evolve. Software development—especially in AI—demands the same comfort with ambiguity and iterative problem-solving.

## What I'm Doing

I'm currently pursuing a **Master's Degree in Artificial Intelligence**, diving into machine learning, neural networks, data science, and the crucial field of AI safety. My focus is on developing practical AI applications that address real challenges in emergency services—from improving training effectiveness to streamlining operational workflows to making incident documentation less burdensome. This blog serves as my learning journal, project portfolio, and a space to explore how my emergency services background might offer unique perspectives on AI challenges.
